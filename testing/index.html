Cock-of-the-rock lek behavior in Mindo

The Andean Cock-of-the-rock is not just a rare splash of orange in the forest. It is a performer. During lek season, multiple males gather at a traditional display arena to call, posture, and compete for attention. Here is what a lek is, how the behavior unfolds, and how to watch it respectfully in the Mindo cloud forest.

When leks happen

A lek is a traditional display site where males gather to compete for female attention. At a Cock-of-the-rock lek, males use calls, posture, wing flicks, hops, and quick chases to establish dominance and attract mates. Individual leks can be used for years, sometimes decades, so visiting one is a special experience.

The most consistent viewing is early in the morning. Arriving before the first calls gives you a better chance to see the full sequence: the initial calling, the build-up, the peak display moments, and the gradual wind-down.

What a lek is (in plain language)

  • Same place, repeated: the display arena is usually on a slope or ridge above a ravine.
  • Multiple males: several males may display in a loose line or cluster, each defending a spot.
  • Female choice: females visit briefly, watch, then select a mate based on display quality and dominance.
  • No feeding here: the behavior is about courtship, not foraging, so the same site can be predictable.

What you will see at a lek

Calling and arrival

Males start calling at first light. You will often hear the lek before you see it.

Display routine

Hops, bowing, wing flicks, and exaggerated posture. Movements are fast and repeated.

Chases and dominance

Short bursts of aggression. A dominant male may push others aside and hold the best perch.

How to watch respectfully

Leks work because the birds feel safe enough to repeat the behavior. The goal is to watch without changing the outcome. If you cause silence, you are too close or too loud.

  1. Arrive early and settle: get into position before the active period begins.
  2. Keep distance: stay back on the trail edge. Let the birds choose to display.
  3. No flash, no playback: both can disrupt behavior and stress individuals.
  4. Limit movement: avoid stepping forward for a better angle. Use binoculars or a longer lens.
  5. Go with a local guide: they know the quiet routes and the correct viewing spots.
Field tip: If you hear calls but do not see birds, watch the mid-level perches along the slope. Displays can happen just out of sight, then shift quickly into view.

FAQ

Do leks happen every day?

Not always. Activity depends on weather, disturbance, and seasonal factors. Calm mornings are best.

How close can I get?

Close enough to see behavior clearly, far enough that calls and displays continue naturally.

Is a guide required?

Not required, but strongly recommended for finding the correct site and watching without disruption.
